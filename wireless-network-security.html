Wireless networks are now the default connection method in homes, enterprises, and public spaces. Unlike wired networks with physical boundaries, wireless signals radiate in all directions — accessible to anyone within range. This fundamental characteristic makes wireless a persistent and high-value attack surface.

You will learn to identify wireless threats, distinguish protocol weaknesses, implement enterprise authentication, and apply hardening controls.

Wireless Attacks

Bluetooth • NFC • RFID • Rogue AP • Evil Twin • RF Jamming • Deauthentication • SY0-701 2.4

The Wireless Attack Surface

Wireless networks create an expanded attack surface because RF signals extend beyond physical boundaries. Multiple wireless technologies coexist in modern environments — each with distinct attack vectors.

Bluetooth Attacks

Bluetooth is a Personal Area Network (PAN) technology using short-range 2.4 GHz RF. When two devices come within range they form a piconet — one device is the parent (master), controlling traffic; others are children (active or parked). Current version: Bluetooth 5.x, which uses ECDH key exchange for Secure Simple Pairing.

📲 Bluejacking

Sends unsolicited messages (text, images, vCards, sounds) to Bluetooth-enabled devices without pairing. No data is stolen — considered more annoying than dangerous. Operates within a few meters. Mitigation: set Bluetooth to non-discoverable; disable when not in use.

🔐 Bluesnarfing

Accesses and copies unauthorized data (emails, contacts, SMS, calendar) from a Bluetooth device without the owner's knowledge. Exploits Bluetooth protocol vulnerabilities to force a connection. A serious data theft attack. Mitigation: keep devices patched; use Bluetooth 5.x with LE Secure Connections.

🔌 Bluebugging

Grants attacker full remote control of a Bluetooth device — making calls, sending messages, eavesdropping, and accessing all data. More severe than bluesnarfing. Exploits older firmware. Mitigation: update firmware; disable Bluetooth in public; use mode 4 (Secure Simple Pairing).

📡 Bluetooth Eavesdropping

Passive capture of Bluetooth traffic. Bluetooth uses frequency hopping spread spectrum (FHSS) — hops across 79 channels up to 1,600 times/second — making it difficult but not impossible to intercept. Application-layer encryption is the key mitigation, as link-layer encryption is not always enforced.

Near Field Communication (NFC) Attacks

NFCNFC (Near Field Communication)Short-range (≤4 cm) wireless standard used for contactless payments, transit cards, access badges, and device pairing. Passive devices (tags) only transmit; active devices can both read and write. The proximity requirement seems secure but relay attacks can extend effective range. enables communication between devices brought within approximately 4 cm. Used in Apple Pay, Google Pay, transit cards, smart locks, and device pairing.

Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) Attacks

RFID is used in employee access badges, inventory tags, supply chain tracking, and library book labels. Most tags are passive (no battery; powered by reader's RF field). The current Generation 2 standard includes security enhancements, but many deployments remain vulnerable.

🔐 Unauthorized Tag Access

A rogue RFID reader can interrogate passive tags without authorization — reading inventory counts, employee badge numbers, or product IDs. No challenge-response needed on many Gen 1 tags. Mitigation: Gen 2 access passwords; shielded enclosures; detect unauthorized readers with RF monitoring.

📞 RFID Cloning

Attacker reads a legitimate access badge and writes the data to a blank RFID card. The cloned badge grants the same physical access as the original. Mitigation: Use high-frequency (HF/UHF) tags with mutual authentication; monitor for anomalous access patterns (duplicate badge use).

📍 RFID Eavesdropping

Passive listening to RFID communications between a legitimate tag and reader. At low frequencies (125 kHz LF), range is very short. At high frequencies (13.56 MHz HF and 860–960 MHz UHF), attacks are more practical. Mitigation: Encrypt RFID payload data; use ISO 15693 or ISO 14443 with authentication.

🚫 Fake Tags / Inventory Fraud

Authentic RFID tags replaced with fakes containing fictitious data — showing products that don't exist, or wrong product info. Undermines inventory integrity. Mitigation: Cryptographic tag authentication; centralized tag registry; physical inspection protocols for high-value items.

Wireless LAN Enterprise Attacks

Wired networks have a well-defined physical perimeter (hard edge). WLANs create blurred edges — RF signals extend through walls into parking lots and adjacent buildings, creating entry points invisible on a network diagram.

Wireless Denial of Service Attacks

Exploits the fact that in early 802.11, management frames (deauth, disassoc) are unencrypted and unauthenticated. An attacker sends forged deauthentication frames impersonating the AP, instantly disconnecting all clients. Tools: aireplay-ng with --deauth flag.

Fix: IEEE 802.11w (Protected Management Frames / PMF) — encrypts and authenticates management frames. Incorporated into 802.11ac; mandatory in WPA3. With PMF required, forged management frames are rejected. This is a key SY0-701 exam topic.

Floods the radio frequency spectrum with noise or competing signals, preventing any device from communicating with the AP. Unlike deauth attacks, RF jamming is a physical-layer attack — it cannot be fixed by firmware. Even common microwave ovens can accidentally jam 2.4 GHz. Intentional jamming is a federal crime (FCC 47 CFR §333).

Mitigation: Use 5 GHz or 6 GHz band (less interference); frequency agility (Dynamic Frequency Selection); WPA3 with PMF; deploy spectrum analyzers to detect jamming sources.

Similar to deauthentication but sends a disassociation frame instead. The client is disassociated from the AP and must re-associate. Often used as a preliminary step to force clients onto an evil twin AP during the re-association window. Also addressed by 802.11w PMF.

The 802.11 frame header contains a Duration field that tells other stations how long the channel will be occupied. An attacker sends frames with an extremely high Duration value, causing all other stations to defer transmission indefinitely — effectively silencing the network. Addressed in newer 802.11 implementations with Duration field validation at the AP.

⚠ SY0-701 — Deauth vs. RF Jamming

These are two distinct attack types. Deauthentication: protocol-level exploit using forged management frames (fixed by software/firmware — 802.11w/PMF). RF Jamming: physical-layer attack using radio interference (cannot be fixed in software; requires physical countermeasures). The exam will ask you to distinguish between them and identify the correct mitigation for each.

Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax) — Security and Performance Advances

🔌 OFDMA — Multi-User Efficiency

Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access divides each Wi-Fi channel into smaller Resource Units. Multiple devices can transmit simultaneously on different sub-channels, dramatically reducing latency in high-density environments (airports, classrooms, stadiums). Reduces contention and idle airtime.

🔋 MU-MIMO (8×8 Up + Downlink)

Wi-Fi 6 extends Multi-User MIMO to 8 simultaneous streams and adds uplink MU-MIMO (Wi-Fi 5 only supported downlink). Doubles throughput in multi-device environments. Combined with OFDMA, drastically improves performance in IoT-dense deployments.

🔒 WPA3 Required

Wi-Fi 6 certification requires WPA3. Wi-Fi 6E (6 GHz band) makes WPA3 and PMF (Protected Management Frames) mandatory. WPA3 eliminates the 4-way handshake capture attack possible with WPA2-PSK and provides per-session forward secrecy. This is why modern wireless deployments must use WPA3.

⏱ Target Wake Time (TWT)

Devices schedule wake-up windows to communicate with the AP, then sleep between transmissions. Reduces battery drain by up to 7x for IoT devices. Security note: TWT scheduling could theoretically allow attackers to predict when a device will be active; properly secured with WPA3 encryption on scheduling frames.

Legacy IEEE 802.11 Security Vulnerabilities

WEP • RC4 • IV Reuse • WPS PIN Flaw • MAC Filtering • SSID Hiding • SY0-701 4.1

Why Legacy Failures Matter

Understanding why WEP, WPS, MAC filtering, and SSID hiding fail explains the design rationale behind WPA3 — and helps you recognize vulnerable configurations in real environments. All of these mechanisms are still found in production networks.

Wired Equivalent Privacy (WEP) — Completely Broken

WEP was designed to provide security equivalent to a wired network. It uses the RC4RC4 (Rivest Cipher 4)A stream cipher that was fast and popular but is now considered broken. WEP's use of RC4 with short, reused keys makes it trivially crackable. RC4 is deprecated and banned in TLS 1.3. stream cipher with a 24-bit Initialization Vector (IV)IV (Initialization Vector)A random value combined with the key to ensure unique ciphertext even when encrypting the same plaintext multiple times. WEP's 24-bit IV is far too short — only 16,777,216 unique values, causing inevitable repeats in busy networks. prepended to a 40-bit or 104-bit shared key.

⚠ The Fatal IV Reuse Attack

WEP uses the same key for every packet — only the IV changes. With only 224 = 16,777,216 possible IVs, a busy wireless network exhausts them in under 7 hours. When two packets share the same IV + key, XOR analysis eliminates the key and exposes plaintext. This violates the fundamental cryptographic rule: never reuse a key+IV combination.

  • Passive collection only — attacker never needs to transmit
  • The FMS attack (Fluhrer, Mantin, Shamir) exploits weak RC4 key scheduling
  • Aircrack-ng cracks WEP in minutes with sufficient IVs
  • Even 128-bit WEP (104-bit key + 24-bit IV) is broken — IV length is the bottleneck
  • CRC-32 integrity check is not cryptographic — can be forged

⚠ WEP Summary of Flaws

  • IV size: 24-bit (16M combinations) — repeats on busy networks in hours
  • Cipher: RC4 with known-weak key scheduling algorithm
  • Integrity: CRC-32 is not a cryptographic MAC — easily forged
  • Key management: Static shared key — compromise affects all users
  • No replay protection: captured packets can be retransmitted
  • No per-user keys: all clients share the same key
Critical — Never Use WEP

WEP provides zero meaningful security. Networks running WEP should be treated as completely open. Any device requiring WEP must be replaced, isolated, or upgraded. The exam will never present WEP as an acceptable security solution.

Wi-Fi Protected Setup (WPS) — Critical Design Flaws

WPS simplifies connecting devices to Wi-Fi. Two common methods: PIN method (8-digit number on router sticker) and Push-button (physical buttons pressed simultaneously). The PIN method has severe, unpatched design vulnerabilities.

⚠ The Three WPS PIN Design Flaws

  1. Last digit is a checksum — derived mathematically from the first 7 digits. Only 7 digits carry real entropy.
  2. Split validation — the router validates the first 4 digits and last 3 digits separately and reports which half is correct. This divides the search space.
  3. No lockout — many implementations allow unlimited PIN attempts without lockout or delay.

Result: 10,000 (first half) + 1,000 (second half) = ~11,000 total attempts to brute-force — achievable in hours with Reaver or Bully.

🔒 WPS Mitigation

Best practice: Disable WPS entirely on all routers and APs. If WPS cannot be disabled, restrict to push-button method only (no PIN exposure). Check if your router has "WPS lockout after N failures" — enable if available. Note: even "disabled" WPS has been found accessible in some router firmware bugs. Verify with a wireless scanner.

MAC Address Filtering — Trivially Defeated

MAC address filtering maintains an allowlist of approved hardware addresses, rejecting connections from unknown MACs. It sounds reasonable — until you understand that MAC addresses are transmitted in plaintext in every 802.11 frame header, before any authentication.

⚠ Why MAC Filtering Fails

  • MAC addresses appear in the clear in every frame header, including before authentication
  • An attacker with a monitor-mode adapter can observe approved MACs in seconds with airodump-ng
  • MAC spoofing is trivial on any OS: ip link set eth0 address XX:XX:XX:XX:XX:XX (Linux), Device Manager (Windows), system preferences (macOS)
  • Once cloned, the attacker connects with an approved identity
  • Large guest/BYOD environments require constant manual MAC management

📌 Real Access Control

MAC filtering is administrative inconvenience, not cryptographic security. Replace it with:

  • WPA3-Enterprise + 802.1X: authenticates the user with unique credentials per-person, not the device hardware
  • Network Access Control (NAC): checks device health, patch level, and certificate — not just MAC
  • Certificate-based auth (EAP-TLS): device certificate cannot be spoofed like a MAC

SSID Hiding — Security Theater

SSID broadcast suppression hides the network name from beacon frames — it doesn't appear in the network list for casual users. But the SSID is still transmitted in several other frame types that any passive monitor can see.

Why SSID Hiding Is Not Security

The SSID still appears in plaintext in: (1) Probe Request frames — clients looking for their preferred hidden network broadcast the SSID by name. (2) Probe Response frames — when the AP responds to a specific probe. (3) Association and Reassociation requests. A passive capture with airodump-ng reveals hidden SSIDs within seconds of any client connecting. Additional downsides: can prevent devices from roaming between APs; harder for guests to connect; provides no actual security. Use WPA3 — not SSID hiding.

WPA — TKIP (Temporal Key Integrity Protocol)

WPA was engineered to run on existing WEP hardware — a transitional fix that replaced WEP's static RC4 key with a dynamically generated per-packet key.

TKIP Improvements Over WEP

  • 128-bit per-packet key — dynamically mixed for each new packet via the TKIP key mixing function
  • Message Integrity Check (MIC) — "Michael" algorithm detects tampering and MITM modifications
  • IV sequencing — 48-bit IV (vs WEP's 24-bit) with strict sequence enforcement prevents replay attacks
  • Still uses RC4 as the underlying cipher — deprecated in IEEE 802.11n and later

WPA PSK Vulnerabilities

  • PSK passphrases shorter than 20 characters are vulnerable to dictionary attacks
  • Key must be shared manually — each new employee / guest requires re-distribution
  • Key change requires updating every device — operationally impractical at scale
  • Single compromise affects all users simultaneously

WPA2 — AES-CCMP Encryption

WPA2 (IEEE 802.11i, 2004) replaced RC4/TKIP with the AES-CCMPAES-CCMPCounter Mode with CBC-MAC Protocol using AES. CCM = Counter Mode (confidentiality via AES block cipher in counter mode) + CBC-MAC (Cipher Block Chaining Message Authentication Code for data integrity/authentication). Both CCMP and TKIP use 128-bit keys and 64-bit MIC values. cipher suite — a fundamental security architecture improvement.

CCM — Confidentiality

Counter Mode (CTR) uses AES as a block cipher operating on 128-bit blocks. Each block encrypted with a unique counter value — eliminates IV reuse. The counter increments for each block, guaranteeing uniqueness without IV size limitations.

CBC-MAC — Integrity & Authentication

Cipher Block Chaining Message Authentication Code provides cryptographic integrity verification and authentication. A 64-bit MIC value covers all key fields in the frame. Any tampering is detected — unlike WEP's CRC-32 which could be forged.

WPA2-PSK 4-Way Handshake Vulnerability

WPA2-Personal's 4-way handshake exchanges hashes that can be captured by a passive attacker. The captured handshake can be brute-forced offline — as fast as the attacker's GPU allows. A 4-character dictionary word may fall in seconds. WPA3's SAE solves this completely.

WPA3 — SAE, GCMP, OWE, and Mandatory PMF

🔒 SAE / Dragonfly Handshake

SAESAE (Simultaneous Authentication of Equals)WPA3's authentication method — also called Dragonfly. Uses a zero-knowledge proof based on elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman. The password is never transmitted; session keys are derived locally on each device. Even full capture of the SAE handshake yields nothing for offline attack. (Simultaneous Authentication of Equals) — also called Dragonfly — replaces the WPA2 4-way handshake. The password is never transmitted or derivable from captured traffic. Session keys are computed locally using ECDH. Perfect Forward Secrecy: each session uses unique keys — compromise of one session doesn't affect others. Immune to offline dictionary attacks.

🔓 GCMP-256 Encryption

GCMPGCMP (Galois/Counter Mode Protocol)WPA3's encryption protocol. Uses AES in Galois/Counter Mode for data confidentiality and GMAC (Galois Message Authentication Code) for integrity. Stronger than CCMP. WPA3-Personal: 128-bit GCMP. WPA3-Enterprise: 256-bit GCMP with CNSA suite (targeting government/high-security use). (Galois/Counter Mode Protocol) replaces CCMP. Uses AES-GCM for encryption and GMAC (Galois Message Authentication Code) for integrity — stronger than CCMP's CBC-MAC. WPA3-Enterprise uses 256-bit GCMP as part of the 192-bit security suite aligned with CNSA (Commercial National Security Algorithm Suite).

🌎 OWE — Encrypted Open Networks

OWEOWE (Opportunistic Wireless Encryption)A WPA3 feature that encrypts open/public Wi-Fi sessions without requiring a password. Each client performs anonymous Diffie-Hellman key exchange with the AP, establishing a unique encrypted session. Prevents passive eavesdropping on coffee shop, hotel, and airport Wi-Fi. (Opportunistic Wireless Encryption) provides individual encrypted sessions on open/passwordless networks. Each device performs an anonymous DH key exchange — unique encrypted session, no password needed. Prevents passive eavesdropping that previously made open Wi-Fi completely transparent to monitoring.

📋 Mandatory PMF

WPA3 mandates Protected Management Frames (802.11w) — encrypting and authenticating deauthentication, disassociation, and channel-switch management frames. Eliminates the deauthentication DoS attack entirely. Previously optional in WPA2; required in all WPA3 deployments and Wi-Fi 6E certification.

Enterprise Wireless Authentication

802.1X • EAP • PEAP • EAP-TLS • EAP-TTLS • EAP-FAST • RADIUS • Captive Portal • SY0-701 4.1

Why Enterprise Authentication?

WPA2/WPA3-Personal requires everyone to share the same passphrase. If one user discloses it, all users lose security. Changing it requires updating every device. WPA2/WPA3-Enterprise solves this with per-user credentials via IEEE 802.1X — each user authenticates independently, and access can be revoked for a single user without affecting others.

IEEE 802.1X — Port-Based Network Access Control

802.1X was originally designed for wired Ethernet but is equally critical for wireless. It blocks all traffic on a port/wireless association until the client successfully authenticates to the back-end authentication server.

EAP-TLS — Most Secure (Mutual Certificate Authentication)

How EAP-TLS Works

  1. Client associates to AP; port blocked
  2. AP sends EAP-Request/Identity
  3. Client sends its digital certificate (not a password)
  4. Server verifies client certificate against trusted CA
  5. Server presents its own certificate to the client
  6. Client verifies server certificate — mutual authentication
  7. TLS session keys derived from handshake
  8. RADIUS returns Access-Accept; AP opens port

Security Assessment

✓ Strongest EAP method

  • Mutual authentication — both sides verified by certificate
  • No passwords — immune to dictionary and phishing attacks
  • Certificate revocation removes compromised devices instantly
  • No offline attacks possible — no secret transmitted

⚠ Deployment Requirements

  • Requires PKI (CA infrastructure) and certificate lifecycle management
  • Client certificate deployment needed on every device
  • Higher administrative overhead vs. PEAP

PEAP — Protected EAP (Most Widely Deployed)

PEAP creates a TLS tunnel using only a server certificate. Inside the tunnel, the client authenticates with username and password (typically MSCHAPv2). Requires no client certificates, using existing Active Directory credentials.

Phase 1 — TLS Tunnel

Server presents its certificate. Client verifies the certificate against a trusted CA. An encrypted TLS tunnel is established. All subsequent authentication traffic is protected inside this tunnel.

Phase 2 — Inner Auth

Inside the TLS tunnel, client authenticates with MSCHAPv2 (username + password). Credentials are protected by the outer TLS encryption. Directly integrates with Active Directory — no PKI rollout needed for clients.

⚠ Critical Configuration Requirement

Clients MUST validate the server certificate. If not configured, an attacker can deploy a rogue RADIUS server with a self-signed certificate, and clients will accept it — exposing MSCHAPv2 credentials. Always pin the specific CA in the client 802.1X supplicant settings.

EAP-TTLS — Tunneled TLS (Maximum Inner Auth Flexibility)

Similar to PEAP: server certificate establishes outer TLS tunnel. The difference is EAP-TTLS supports any inner authentication method including PAP, CHAP, MS-CHAP, MS-CHAPv2, and other EAP types — making it more flexible than PEAP in heterogeneous environments.

Advantages Over PEAP

  • Supports legacy inner auth methods (PAP, CHAP) inside TLS — enables migration from legacy systems
  • Cross-platform: better native support on Linux/Android vs. PEAP
  • Used in FreeRADIUS deployments with diverse client types

Deployment Notes

  • Same server certificate validation requirement as PEAP
  • If using PAP inner auth, MSCHAPv2 offline crack risk is reduced — but PAP transmits passwords in plaintext inside TLS (still protected by outer tunnel)
  • Less native Windows supplicant support than PEAP

EAP-FAST — Flexible Authentication via Secure Tunneling

Cisco-developed to replace LEAP (which had a fatal MSCHAPv2 flaw allowing offline password recovery). EAP-FAST uses Protected Access Credentials (PAC) — pre-provisioned credential files — instead of server certificates to establish the TLS tunnel.

PAC-Based Tunneling

PAC files contain a shared secret between the client and RADIUS server. Phase 0: PAC provisioning (in-band or out-of-band). Phase 1: TLS tunnel established using PAC. Phase 2: inner authentication. No PKI certificates required — lowers deployment barrier. Primarily used in Cisco-centric environments.

Security Consideration

Anonymous in-band PAC provisioning (Phase 0 without authentication) creates a temporary unprotected tunnel during provisioning — potentially exploitable. Best practice: use authenticated PAC provisioning (out-of-band) or restrict MAC addresses during provisioning. Verify RADIUS server identity in PAC provisioning phase.

Captive Portal Authentication

A captive portalCaptive PortalAn AP type that intercepts a new wireless client's first web request and redirects it to an authentication or policy acceptance page. The network port remains blocked until the user authenticates or accepts terms. Common in hotels, coffee shops, airports, and enterprise guest networks. AP intercepts the first web request from an unauthenticated client and redirects them to a login/policy page.

Captive Portal Use Cases

  • Hotel and coffee shop internet access (terms acceptance)
  • Enterprise guest network isolation
  • Airport / venue public hotspots
  • Education institution guest/student onboarding
  • Event networks with single-use credentials

Captive Portal Risks

  • Traffic before portal authentication is unencrypted on open networks
  • HTTP portal pages expose credentials to interception
  • Evil twin can serve a fake captive portal to steal credentials
  • Combine with WPA3/OWE to encrypt traffic before and after portal auth
  • Always use HTTPS-only portals with valid certificates

Wireless Network Hardening

Rogue AP Detection • AP Types • Site Survey • Antenna Placement • Power Controls • WIDS/WIPS

Beyond Encryption: Comprehensive Wireless Security

Strong encryption and authentication (WPA3 + 802.1X) form the cryptographic foundation, but a fully hardened wireless network also requires rogue device detection, proper physical AP deployment, architectural segmentation, and wireless peripheral protection.

Wireless Hardening Checklist

  • Deploy WPA3 (or WPA2-Enterprise at minimum) — disable WEP, WPA, and open networks entirely
  • Disable WPS on all access points and wireless routers — especially PIN method
  • Enable Protected Management Frames (PMF / 802.11w) — set to Required to block deauthentication attacks
  • Deploy Wireless IDS/IPS (WIDS/WIPS) with rogue AP detection probes covering all areas
  • Segment guest wireless from corporate wireless on separate VLANs with firewall between them
  • Conduct site survey — map RF coverage, identify dead zones, measure signal bleed beyond perimeter
  • Adjust AP transmit power — minimize signal reaching the parking lot or adjacent buildings
  • Position APs near center of coverage area, mounted high on walls to reduce obstructions and theft
  • Keep all AP firmware patched and current — many wireless attacks exploit unpatched firmware CVEs
  • Change all default AP admin credentials to strong, unique passwords or use certificate-based admin auth
  • Replace wireless mice and keyboards vulnerable to MouseJack/KeySweeper injection attacks with patched models or wired peripherals in high-security areas

Rogue AP Detection — Four Probe Types

Detecting unauthorized access points requires continuous RF monitoring. When a suspicious AP is detected, information is sent to a centralized WLAN management database, which compares it against the authorized AP list. Any unrecognized AP triggers an alert.

📲 Wireless Device Probe

Software running on end-user devices (laptops, phones) that continuously scans for APs while the device is operating normally. Lightweight and distributed — every device in the organization contributes to wireless coverage monitoring. Limited to areas where devices are actively being used.

💻 Desktop Probe

Dedicated software running continuously on fixed desktops or workstations with a monitor-mode WLAN adapter. Provides persistent scanning at fixed office locations. Feeds detection data to the centralized WIDS management console. More reliable than device probes — always on and stationary.

🔌 Access Point Probe

Enterprise APs configured to simultaneously serve clients and scan for unauthorized transmissions on non-serving channels. AP periodically sweeps through channels looking for unknown BSSIDs. No additional hardware required — uses existing infrastructure. Managed and consolidated through the Wireless LAN Controller (WLC).

📌 Dedicated Probe

Hardware sensors deployed solely for RF monitoring — no client service. Provides full-spectrum, continuous monitoring on all channels simultaneously. Highest detection capability and lowest impact on client performance. Deployed in high-security areas: server rooms, executive floors, data centers, R&D labs.

Access Point Types

Fat APs (also called autonomous APs) contain all the intelligence required to independently manage wireless authentication, encryption, QoS, channel selection, and other functions. Self-contained — no controller required. Each AP is configured and managed individually.

Security implications: Each AP must be patched and configured separately — high administrative burden at scale. Inconsistent configurations across APs are a common security risk in fat AP deployments. Suitable for small deployments (home, small branch office) where centralized management is not feasible.

Thin APs (lightweight APs) offload management, authentication, and encryption functions to a centralized Wireless LAN Controller (WLC). The WLC is the single point of configuration — changes propagate to all managed APs automatically.

Security benefits: Consistent policy enforcement across all APs. Centralized authentication (handoff between APs is seamless — no re-authentication needed). Integrated rogue AP detection through the WLC. Simplified firmware management — update once, apply everywhere. Centralized logging to SIEM. Strongly preferred for enterprise deployments.

Captive portal APs redirect a connecting client's first web request to an authentication or policy acceptance page using a standard browser. No special client software required. Common in hotels, coffee shops, airports, and enterprise guest networks.

Security considerations: Traffic before portal authentication is often unencrypted on open networks — combine with WPA3/OWE. Use HTTPS-only portals with valid CA certificates. Isolate captive portal users behind NAT — they should not be able to reach the corporate network. Log and monitor portal activity. Consider time-limited sessions and bandwidth throttling for guest access.

A wireless site survey is an in-depth examination of the physical environment to optimize AP placement, channel assignments, and power levels. Both passive surveys (listen only) and active surveys (connect and measure) provide valuable data.

Security-relevant survey goals:

  • Signal containment: measure how far the signal extends beyond the building perimeter; reduce transmit power to minimize external exposure
  • Channel planning: 2.4 GHz non-overlapping channels: 1, 6, 11; 5 GHz has many more options reducing interference
  • Antenna placement: mount high and centrally; use directional antennas along building edges to contain coverage inward
  • Dead zone identification: ensure no legitimate coverage gaps that could force users to connect to unauthorized APs

Key Exam Topics Summary

Must-Know Protocol Facts

WEP = RC4 + 24-bit IV (broken). WPA = TKIP (RC4, deprecated). WPA2 = AES-CCMP (acceptable). WPA3 = GCMP-256 + SAE (current). Bluejacking sends messages only. Bluesnarfing steals data. Rogue AP = insider; Evil Twin = external attacker with same SSID.

Critical Comparisons

EAP-TLS (certs both sides) vs. PEAP (server cert only). RF jamming (physical layer, spectrum noise) vs. deauth attack (protocol layer, forged frames, fixed by 802.11w). WPA3-SAE (no offline attack) vs. WPA2-PSK (4-way handshake capturable). SSID hiding vs. actual WPA3 encryption. MAC filtering vs. 802.1X auth.

Common Exam Traps

WPS PIN is always vulnerable — only push-button is safer. SSID hiding provides zero real security. MAC addresses are sent in plaintext — easily spoofed. WPA2-PSK 4-way handshake CAN be captured and cracked offline (WPA3 SAE prevents this). PMF is optional in WPA2 but MANDATORY in WPA3. 802.11w IS the PMF standard.
